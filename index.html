Abstract
Research contribution

The gap: Existing AI infrastructure observability tools treat cost, carbon, and pipeline efficiency as separate optimization problems — siloed across FinOps dashboards, sustainability reports, and MLOps monitoring systems. No unified decision layer exists that enables simultaneous cost-carbon-pipeline trade-off analysis at the business decision level.


This framework: An integrated observability methodology that joins production inference cost data, training pipeline cost and failure telemetry, real CPU utilization from infrastructure monitoring, and verified grid carbon intensity data into a unified view. A Software Carbon Intensity (SCI) score — per the Green Software Foundation specification — is calculated for inference workloads using real operational data and modeled embodied carbon from the Cloud Carbon Footprint dataset.


Key findings: Applied to one year of production AI infrastructure data from a SaaS environment (41,782 inference cost records; 10,515 pipeline job records), the framework surfaces three novel decision opportunities: (1) a 232× carbon intensity gap between the lowest and highest-carbon deployment regions, driven by grid composition differences between hydroelectric and fossil-fuel-dominant markets; (2) an ~18% pipeline failure rate representing ~$110K and ~42,000 kg CO₂ annually in waste with zero business output; (3) a retraining cadence trade-off that reduces carbon emissions by 45% relative to weekly retraining with no model quality degradation.


Contribution: The framework demonstrates that decision-grade carbon estimates — using real utilization telemetry, verified government grid intensity data, and published embodied carbon coefficients — are sufficient to surface materially different infrastructure decisions than cost-only or carbon-only analysis. This is validated through practitioner engagement with sustainability and cloud economics stakeholders at the subject organization.

The dominant inference deployment region (us-east-2 · Columbus OH) operates on a grid with 394 gCO₂/kWh per EPA eGRID 2024 RFCW subregion — a mix primarily of natural gas and coal. The Canadian region (ca-central-1 · Montreal) operates at 1.7 gCO₂/kWh — Quebec's electrical grid is 99.8% hydroelectric per Canada's National Inventory Report 2024.[6]

This is not a marginal difference. The same inference workload produces 232× the carbon emissions in Columbus vs. Montreal. The Canadian region already handles 6% of observed inference compute ($60K/yr) — the framework surfaces the question of which additional workloads are eligible for routing based on latency requirements.

Portland OR (us-west-2) at 94 gCO₂/kWh (EPA eGRID NWPP — predominantly hydroelectric and wind) represents a lower-carbon option for US-latency-constrained workloads.

Carbon framing of pipeline waste
The SCI denominator problem · R = 0 for failed jobs

In the SCI specification, R is the functional unit — the useful output the software produces. For a failed pipeline job, R = 0. The numerator (E × I + M) is unchanged — energy was consumed, carbon was emitted, hardware embodied carbon was amortized.


This makes pipeline failure rate a direct SCI lever: reducing failure rate from 18% to 5% improves effective SCI by reducing wasted carbon in the numerator — without changing the per-successful-request score, simply by eliminating carbon expenditure that produces nothing.


Zombie tasks — jobs that are broken but remain on recurring schedules — represent a second layer where R = 0 indefinitely. Quantifying this requires pipeline instrumentation not currently available in the observed data sources.

06 — SCI sensitivity analysis · what moves the score most
Carbon reduction levers · ranked by estimated impact on SCI score

↑ Highest impact · zero infrastructure cost
Grid routing
Columbus → Montreal reduces E×I term by 232×. Batch and non-latency-sensitive workloads are candidates. Portland (94g) reduces by 4× for US-latency-constrained work. Highest ROI carbon intervention available — requires routing config change only.

actionable now
↑ High impact · requires instrumentation
Failure rate
18% pipeline failure = ~$110K and ~42K kg CO₂ annually with R = 0. Zombie tasks add an uncaptured additional layer. Reducing failure rate from 18% to 5% recovers ~72% of wasted training carbon. Requires pipeline monitoring instrumentation not currently in place.

pipeline instrumentation needed
↑ Medium impact · confirm then migrate
Graviton migration
c5 → c7g Graviton: ~18% lower M term (embodied carbon) + ~20% lower E term (operational energy). TensorFlow supports ARM. Compounding reduction — both SCI terms improve simultaneously. Requires cluster migration, no model changes.

instance type unconfirmed
↑ Medium impact · already measured
Utilization
11.1% CPU utilization means 88.9% of provisioned compute is idle. Over-provisioning is a carbon tax amortized across every request. Each 1% utilization increase reduces per-request carbon proportionally. Right-sizing is a carbon intervention, not just a cost intervention.

measured · Datadog telemetry
~ Lower impact · audit-grade refinement
PUE accuracy
Using AWS regional PUE average of 1.2. AWS publishes actual per-region PUE figures — exact values change E term by ~5-10%. Relevant for audit-grade carbon accounting; not the priority for decision-making accuracy at this stage.

refinement
✦ Future state · high potential
Real-time grid scheduling
Electricity Maps API[10] provides hourly marginal grid intensity. Scheduling batch inference and training during low-carbon grid periods — high renewable generation windows — could reduce I term by 20-40% with no infrastructure change. Requires live data pipeline integration.
